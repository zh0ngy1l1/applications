I spent an unforgettable month this past summer attending the Yau Mathcamp in Shanghai. I sought an experience closer to real mathematics research, in contrast to my mathematical contest endeavours, so that I could finally decide whether to further pursue the subject or study something else instead. The camp not only gave me the confidence to practise mathematics, but also let me experience research for myself, a joy I had not expected before college.

The problem sheets accompanying daily lectures always had a gradient of difficulty, each containing several tricky problems that I would not solve alone. When necessary, I asked for hints, discussed approaches with friends, and orally presented my solutions to the professor. I immersed myself in learning new areas of mathematics on a schedule familiar from olympiad training camps, and it felt that studying mathematics at university would not be too intense to handle, but more thrilling thanks to the higher level of difficulty.

The highlight of this camp for me was not the teaching itself, but the chance to work on a Mathcamp Project. My team of three spent several hours daily researching a problem in Diophantine approximation, a topic first introduced to us at the camp. This was also my first time reading academic articles in mathematics. I was wholly devoted to the project, empowered by the knowledge that our work was truly novel. This motivated us to finalise our results into a presentation earning Gold Prize. Though we struggled against the scarcity of time, the thrill of sharing our results with a wider audience kept us going.

The camp broadened my view beyond the limited scope of olympiad problems that had defined my perception of mathematics, and showed me that studying mathematics in college would fulfill my dream of the summer camp lasting much longer.
